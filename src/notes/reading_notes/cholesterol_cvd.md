# Cholesterol, ApoB, insulin, glucose, keto, and atherosclerosis

Population evidence synthesis. Not personal medical advice.

Redo status: 2026-06-13. This version used the long-running browser session and the required Perplexity, Gemini, and Google Scholar routes. Local search artifacts and paper text are under `cholesterol_cvd_papers/`.

## Bottom line

The strongest evidence supports this model: ApoB-containing lipoproteins are causal upstream material for ordinary atherosclerotic plaque; insulin resistance, hyperglycemia, blood pressure, inflammation, kidney disease, smoking, and thrombosis alter the rate and danger of that plaque process.

The old phrase "cholesterol clogs arteries" is crude but not directionally empty. The precise claim is not that free cholesterol passively plugs pipes. The evidence-based claim is that LDL, remnants, and other ApoB-containing particles enter and are retained in the arterial wall; immune, smooth-muscle, thrombotic, and calcific responses then create plaque, stenosis, rupture risk, and events.

The keto claim has a true part and an overreach. True: low-carb diets can improve weight, HbA1c, triglycerides, HDL-C, insulin resistance markers, blood pressure, and medication burden in some people, especially type 2 diabetes populations. Overreach: those improvements do not prove that very high LDL-C or ApoB is harmless when glucose and insulin markers look favorable.

Best current read: metabolic context matters, but it is not a shield that makes ApoB exposure irrelevant. The lean keto hyper-responder subgroup is worth studying; it is not yet an exception that overturns the broader causal LDL/ApoB evidence.

## Evidence map

| claim | evidence strength | read |
|---|---:|---|
| LDL/ApoB-containing particles cause ASCVD | very strong | mechanism, genetics, prospective cohorts, Mendelian randomization, and randomized LDL-lowering trials agree |
| ApoB can be a better risk marker than LDL-C when markers disagree | strong | ApoB tracks atherogenic particle number; LDL-C tracks cholesterol mass |
| Lowering LDL-C/ApoB lowers events | very strong | statins, ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitors, bempedoic acid point the same way |
| Diabetes/insulin resistance accelerates atherosclerosis | strong | endothelial dysfunction, glycation, inflammation, triglyceride-rich remnants, thrombosis |
| Intensive glucose lowering alone reliably prevents hard ASCVD events in established T2D | mixed | nonfatal MI signals exist; mortality/CV death effects are not clean in older RCT meta-analyses |
| Low-carb diets can improve short-term T2D remission and metabolic markers | moderate | effect often shrinks by 12 months; adherence and food quality matter |
| High LDL-C/ApoB is safe in lean keto hyper-responders | unproven | imaging studies are small/selected; no hard-outcome evidence |

## Fact, inference, uncertainty

Fact: the 2017 EAS consensus conclusion says LDL "causes ASCVD." Its evidence base includes genetic, epidemiologic, Mendelian randomization, and randomized-trial evidence.

Fact: the 2020 EAS pathophysiology consensus describes LDL as the "key deliverer of cholesterol" to the artery wall and centers retention/modification in the intima.

Fact: the 2024 NLA ApoB consensus says ApoB "represents the total concentration" of atherogenic particles and is "superior to LDL-C" for risk assessment before and during lipid-lowering therapy.

Fact: the CTT meta-analysis found major vascular events fell with LDL reduction; its interpretation says each 1 mmol/L LDL-C reduction lowers major vascular events by "just over a fifth."

Fact: high glucose can chemically modify LDL. A 2023 review states: "High glucose levels in blood can result in glycation" of lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

Inference: glucose/insulin status modifies risk per person and probably risk per plaque environment. It does not remove the causal role of retained ApoB particles.

Uncertainty: long-term event risk in lean mass hyper-responders with very high LDL-C/ApoB, low triglycerides, high HDL-C, low HbA1c, and low blood pressure is not established.

## Vessel blockage model

The pipe-clog story is misleading because atherosclerosis is a living tissue process.

Better mechanism:

- ApoB-containing particles cross the endothelium
- they are retained by arterial-wall matrix, especially proteoglycans
- retained particles are modified by oxidation, glycation, aggregation, and enzymatic processes
- macrophages, smooth-muscle cells, inflammatory mediators, and extracellular matrix form plaque
- plaque can narrow the vessel, rupture, erode, thrombose, calcify, or remodel outward without obvious early narrowing

This explains why LDL/ApoB can be causal even when events are not just mechanical blockage. Events often involve plaque vulnerability and thrombosis, not only percent stenosis.

## ApoB versus LDL-C

LDL-C is cholesterol mass inside LDL particles. ApoB approximates the number of atherogenic particles because each LDL, VLDL remnant, IDL, and Lp(a) particle carries one ApoB molecule.

When LDL-C and ApoB are concordant, they usually tell the same story. When they are discordant, ApoB usually better captures particle burden. This matters for insulin resistance because triglyceride-rich, small, cholesterol-depleted particles can make LDL-C understate ApoB burden. It also matters for keto hyper-responders because very high LDL-C commonly implies high LDL particle number or high ApoB unless proven otherwise.

Practical inference for this research question: a claim that "LDL-C is high but harmless" is weak unless it addresses ApoB/LDL-P, Lp(a), blood pressure, smoking, kidney disease, inflammation, diabetes status, age, plaque imaging, and follow-up duration.

## Glucose and insulin

The strongest pro-insulin/glucose argument is that atherosclerosis is worse in insulin resistance and diabetes for reasons beyond LDL-C. Hyperglycemia can glycate LDL and vascular proteins; insulin resistance can raise triglyceride-rich lipoproteins and remnants; diabetes increases endothelial dysfunction, inflammation, oxidative stress, thrombosis, kidney disease, and hypertension.

That argument is important but does not replace ApoB. In diabetes, LDL-C can be deceptively ordinary while ApoB/remnant burden and vascular injury are high. Statin meta-analysis evidence in diabetes still shows vascular event reduction per LDL-C lowering.

The strongest counterpoint to a glucose-only theory is randomized evidence. Older intensive glucose-lowering trials in T2D did not cleanly reduce mortality or cardiovascular death. A BMJ meta-analysis found no significant effect on all-cause mortality or CV death, while nonfatal MI decreased and severe hypoglycemia increased. This means glycemia is important, but "lower glucose alone fixes ASCVD risk" is too simple.

Modern GLP-1 receptor agonist and SGLT2 inhibitor outcome trials add nuance: cardiovascular and kidney benefits exist, but they are not merely proof that insulin/glucose is the only causal pathway. Weight, blood pressure, renal hemodynamics, inflammation, heart failure biology, and other mechanisms are involved.

## Keto and low-carb evidence

Strongest keto-side points:

- Low-carb diets can improve short-term diabetes remission, weight, triglycerides, and insulin sensitivity
- the 2021 BMJ meta-analysis found higher T2D remission at 6 months, with benefits less durable at 12 months
- the 2019 NLA statement says low-CHO diets can help appetite, triglycerides, and T2D medication reduction
- lean mass hyper-responders appear to be a real phenotype: the 2022 paper reports BMI was inversely associated with LDL-C change, and low TG/HDL predicted larger LDL-C increases
- the 2024 KETO Trial found no significant difference in coronary plaque burden in 80 keto hyper-responders versus matched controls

Strongest counterpoints:

- the KETO Trial is small, selected, and cross-sectional/matched; it is not a hard-event or progression trial
- the KETO Trial itself notes exceptionally high LDL-C is usually coincident with high LDL-P and ApoB
- the 2025 longitudinal KETO-CTA paper should not be used; the 2026 retraction says methodology concerns affect "the reliability of the data"
- a controlled feeding trial in normal-weight women found LDL-C increased in every participant and ApoB increased
- a 2024 UK Biobank LCHF analysis found higher LDL-C/ApoB and higher incident MACE; this is observational and confounded, but it cuts against assuming low-carb context is protective enough
- the 2019 NLA low-carb statement says LDL-C effects are mixed, some studies show increases, and long-term safety data are minimal

Current inference: low-carb can reduce several risk accelerants while raising ApoB in some people. The open question is whether those improvements offset high ApoB in a narrow lean subgroup over decades. The current evidence does not establish that they do.

## What would change the conclusion

Evidence that would materially strengthen the keto exception:

- large prospective LMHR cohorts with measured ApoB/LDL-P, Lp(a), HbA1c, insulin resistance, diet composition, and adjudicated ASCVD events
- CCTA or coronary MRI progression studies with baseline pre-diet imaging and repeated imaging
- analyses showing low insulin/TG/high HDL materially lowers event risk per ApoB particle
- mechanistic evidence that keto-induced ApoB particles are retained less in human arterial wall at the same ApoB concentration

Evidence that would weaken the keto exception:

- progression of noncalcified plaque in LMHR cohorts despite low insulin/glucose markers
- event rates tracking ApoB exposure after adjustment for keto metabolic improvements
- consistent ApoB/LDL-P elevation in high LDL-C keto responders
- trial evidence that lowering ApoB in this subgroup improves plaque or events

## Current synthesis

The most defensible position is multi-causal:

- ApoB exposure is causal substrate
- insulin resistance and hyperglycemia are accelerants
- inflammation, blood pressure, kidney disease, smoking, and thrombosis are accelerants
- diet quality changes several pathways at once
- low-carb benefits are real for some endpoints and populations
- very high LDL-C/ApoB on keto remains an unresolved risk, not a proven safe state

So the answer to the original controversy is: cholesterol/ApoB-containing particles do contribute to atherosclerotic vessel disease, but the old one-factor clogging story is incomplete. Insulin resistance/high glucose can make the process worse and can explain why risk differs between people with the same LDL-C. They do not establish that high ApoB is benign.
