# Cholesterol, ApoB, glucose, insulin, and atherosclerosis

Population-level research synthesis, not medical advice.

## Bottom line

The strongest evidence says LDL/ApoB-containing particles are causal in atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. The evidence is not just an old diet-heart story: it comes from vessel-wall biology, genetic studies, prospective cohorts, Mendelian randomization, and randomized trials where lowering LDL-C/ApoB lowers events.

The insulin/high-blood-sugar claim contains a true part and an overreach. Hyperglycemia, insulin resistance, inflammation, blood pressure, triglyceride-rich remnants, and glycation can make vessels and lipoproteins more atherogenic. That supports an interaction model: metabolic disease can amplify risk. It does not establish that high LDL-C or high ApoB is harmless when glucose and insulin are low.

The keto-specific evidence is the weakest part. Low-carb interventions can improve HbA1c, triglycerides, HDL-C, blood pressure, and inflammatory markers, especially in type 2 diabetes. Some lean low-carb people develop very high LDL-C/ApoB with low triglycerides and high HDL-C. Small imaging and observational studies raise real uncertainty about risk in this subgroup, but they do not overturn the general causal evidence.

## Evidence map

| claim | evidence strength | read |
|---|---:|---|
| LDL/ApoB-containing particles cause atherosclerosis | very strong | genetic + RCT + mechanistic agreement |
| Lowering LDL-C lowers ASCVD events | very strong | CTT statin meta-analysis, ezetimibe, PCSK9, bempedoic acid |
| LDL-C lowering reduces events in diabetes | strong | CTT diabetes meta-analysis |
| ApoB/particle burden clarifies risk when lipid markers disagree | strong | UK Biobank + trial-population analyses |
| Hyperglycemia/insulin resistance worsen vascular biology | strong | glycation, oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, remnant/LDL retention |
| Intensive glucose lowering alone reliably prevents major ASCVD events in T2D | mixed | modest/nonfatal MI signal; mortality and CV death not clearly reduced in older RCT meta-analyses |
| Keto/low-carb improves several cardiometabolic markers | moderate | best in T2D/obesity; less clear in lean normal-weight people |
| High LDL-C/ApoB is safe in lean keto/LMHR because insulin/glucose are low | unproven | plausible modifiers, but no hard-outcome evidence and limited imaging data |

## Marker map

| marker | what it captures | useful for this question | main caveat |
|---|---|---|---|
| LDL-C | cholesterol mass inside LDL particles | trial target and common exposure measure | can misstate particle number when cholesterol per particle varies |
| ApoB | number of ApoB-containing atherogenic particles | closest routine proxy for particle burden | not identical to plaque or event risk |
| Non-HDL-C | cholesterol in all non-HDL particles | broader than LDL-C; includes remnant cholesterol | still cholesterol mass, not particle count |
| LDL-P | LDL particle number | can show LDL-C/particle discordance | less standardized and less available than ApoB |
| Triglycerides/remnants | TG-rich lipoproteins and metabolic dyslipidemia | important in insulin resistance and diabetes | TG level is an imperfect remnant-particle measure |
| HDL-C | cholesterol in HDL particles | low HDL-C often marks insulin resistance | raising HDL-C itself has not proved a clean event-reduction strategy |
| HbA1c/insulin resistance | glycemic and metabolic environment | modifies absolute vascular risk and lipoprotein behavior | does not replace ApoB exposure as plaque substrate |
| CAC/CCTA plaque | imaging evidence of calcified and/or anatomic plaque | useful surrogate for existing burden | not a hard event endpoint and may miss future exposure effects |
| Hard events | MI, stroke, revascularization, CV death | most decision-relevant outcome class | needs long follow-up and large cohorts/trials |

## LDL/ApoB causal evidence

Fact: atherosclerosis begins when ApoB-containing lipoproteins enter and are retained in the arterial wall. The 2020 EAS consensus describes the process as LDL retention and modification in the intima; its mechanistic section says the atherogenicity of LDL is linked to apoB100 binding arterial wall proteoglycans.

Fact: the 2017 EAS consensus conclusion says: "LDL causes ASCVD." It bases that on clinical, genetic, epidemiologic, Mendelian randomization, and intervention evidence.

Guideline context: the 2025 ESC/EAS focused update was tracked as the current update to the 2019 dyslipidaemia guideline. The official ESC page is in the local source trail, but direct local fetch of the official OUP article was blocked, so the causal support here rests on the open EAS consensus statements and outcome trials rather than on a mirrored guideline PDF.

Fact: intervention evidence is dose-consistent. The CTT meta-analysis of 170,000 participants found major vascular events fell with LDL-C reduction, with RR 0.78 per 1 mmol/L LDL-C reduction. Its interpretation says each 1 mmol/L reduction lowers major vascular events "by just over a fifth."

Fact: non-statin LDL lowering also reduces events. IMPROVE-IT found lower events when ezetimibe was added to simvastatin after ACS, HR 0.936. FOURIER found evolocumab lowered LDL-C 59% and reduced the primary endpoint, HR 0.85. ODYSSEY OUTCOMES found alirocumab reduced recurrent ischemic events after ACS, HR 0.85. In CLEAR Outcomes primary-prevention subgroup, bempedoic acid lowered LDL-C by 21.3% and reduced the primary endpoint, HR 0.70.

Fact: ApoB helps separate particle burden from cholesterol mass. Marston et al. found MI risk was "best captured by the number of apoB-containing lipoproteins." Sniderman et al. concluded LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and triglycerides are "not adequate proxies for apoB" when discordant.

Inference: this is why the mainstream view treats ApoB particle exposure over time as a cause, not merely a marker. LDL-C is an estimate of cholesterol mass inside LDL particles; ApoB better approximates the number of atherogenic particles when LDL-C and particle count are discordant.

## Glucose and insulin

Fact: diabetes and insulin resistance raise ASCVD risk through many channels: hyperglycemia, oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, inflammation, hypertension, kidney disease, thrombosis, and atherogenic dyslipidemia.

Fact: LDL lowering still reduces vascular events in diabetes. The CTT diabetes meta-analysis found a "significant 21% proportional reduction" in major vascular events per 1 mmol/L LDL-C reduction in people with diabetes, similar to people without diabetes.

Fact: hyperglycemia can chemically modify LDL. A 2023 review states: "High glucose levels in blood can result in glycation" of lipids and proteins, and reports elevated AGE-modified LDL in diabetes.

Fact: insulin resistance may increase arterial retention of ApoB particles. A 2025 review summarizes experimental work where insulin-resistant rats had 3.6-fold higher LDL retention and 2.8-fold higher remnant retention than lean controls.

Fact: older intensive HbA1c-lowering outcome evidence is not a simple proof that glucose is the master switch. A BMJ meta-analysis of intensive glucose control in T2D found no significant effect on all-cause mortality or CV death, while reducing nonfatal MI and microalbuminuria and increasing severe hypoglycemia.

Fact: modern diabetes therapies complicate the story. SGLT2 inhibitor and GLP-1 receptor agonist outcome trials show cardiovascular and kidney benefits in T2D, but those benefits are not simply "lower glucose fixes vessels"; they appear to involve heart failure, kidney, weight, blood pressure, inflammation, and other mechanisms.

Current guideline context: the 2026 ADA cardiovascular risk standards were saved for diabetes context. They are not used here to give individual treatment advice.

Inference: glucose/insulin status modifies the background environment in which ApoB particles act. It likely changes plaque initiation/progression rate, plaque vulnerability, and competing risks. It is not evidence that ApoB particles stop being atherogenic.

## Keto and low-carb claims

Strongest keto-side points:

- Low-carb diets can improve short-term diabetes remission, weight, triglycerides, and insulin sensitivity in some T2D trials. A 2021 BMJ meta-analysis found higher remission at 6 months and improvements that diminished by 12 months; it also noted possible LDL-C worsening at 12 months.
- The 2019 National Lipid Association statement says low-CHO diets may help appetite, triglycerides, and medication reduction in T2D, but are not superior for weight loss, have mixed LDL-C effects, and lack long-term safety data.
- In the Virta open-label T2D intervention, LDL-C rose but ApoB was not significantly changed at 1 year, while small LDL-P, TG/HDL, blood pressure, and hsCRP improved.
- The LMHR phenotype is real enough to study. The 2022 paper found LDL-C rises were larger in leaner people with low TG/HDL markers; its abstract says "BMI was inversely associated" with LDL-C change.
- A 2022 LMHR case report found no plaque on CTA after more than 2 years of very high LDL-C, but it was one subject.
- The 2024 KETO Trial imaging study found "no significant difference in coronary plaque burden" in 80 keto hyper-responders versus matched controls, despite mean LDL-C 272 mg/dL and mean keto duration 4.7 years.

Strongest counterpoints:

- The KETO Trial is not an outcomes trial and not a progression trial. It is small, selected, cross-sectional/matched imaging. It cannot show lifetime safety or event risk.
- The KETO Trial authors note that exceptionally high LDL-C is necessarily coincident with high LDL-P/ApoB. That weakens the idea that high LDL-C is just harmless cholesterol mass.
- The longitudinal KETO-CTA paper was retracted in May 2026, after an expression of concern. Do not use it as evidence that ApoB is safe in LMHR.
- A 2021 controlled feeding trial in healthy normal-weight women found the LCHF diet increased LDL-C in every participant and increased ApoB, small dense LDL, and large buoyant LDL.
- A 2024 UK Biobank analysis found LCHF participants had higher LDL-C and ApoB and higher incident MACE, HR 2.18 after adjustment. This is observational and confounded, but it points against assuming low-carb context is protective enough.
- A 2021 ketogenic-diet case series warned that high-fat low-carb diets can cause or exacerbate marked hypercholesterolemia in people with or without known genetic hyperlipidemia.

Inference: low-carb metabolic improvements can reduce several major risk pathways, but whether they offset very high ApoB in lean hyper-responders is not known. The current evidence supports "metabolic context matters" but not "cholesterol is fine on keto." Do not generalize LMHR findings to people with diabetes, metabolic syndrome, familial hypercholesterolemia, established ASCVD, kidney disease, older age, or high baseline risk.

## How to reason about the controversy

The phrase "cholesterol clogs arteries" is shorthand and should not be the central claim. More precise: ApoB-containing lipoproteins enter, are retained, and modified in the arterial wall; immune and smooth-muscle responses turn that into plaque.

The newer keto claim is useful as a challenge to simplistic risk calculators because a lean low-carb person with LDL-C 250, HDL-C 90, TG 50, low HbA1c, low hsCRP, and low blood pressure is not metabolically equivalent to a person with diabetes, hypertension, high TG, and the same LDL-C.

The overreach is treating favorable glucose/insulin markers as a shield against ApoB exposure. The causal LDL/ApoB evidence is broader than insulin-resistant populations, and most event trials support lower ApoB exposure across risk backgrounds.

Best current synthesis: ASCVD risk is multi-causal. ApoB exposure is a required upstream substrate for typical atherosclerotic plaque; insulin resistance and hyperglycemia are accelerants; inflammation and blood pressure are additional accelerants; low-carb can improve some accelerants but may raise ApoB in some people.

## Evidence-class limitations

- LDL/ApoB causality: strongest evidence class because mechanisms, genetics, prospective cohorts, Mendelian randomization, and RCTs point the same way. Remaining uncertainty is more about individual absolute risk and marker discordance than about the direction of causal effect.
- Glycemia/insulin: strong risk-modifier evidence, especially in diabetes and insulin resistance. This evidence supports acceleration and interaction, not replacement of retained ApoB-containing particles as plaque substrate.
- Diet-pattern evidence: useful for HbA1c, weight, TG, HDL-C, blood pressure, medication burden, and LDL/ApoB response, but many diet trials are short, adherence is difficult, food substitutions differ, and hard events are sparse.
- Keto/LMHR evidence: hypothesis-generating. Case reports, selected cohorts, and CAC/CCTA imaging can raise uncertainty, but they are not reassuring proof of long-term safety from very high LDL-C/ApoB exposure.

## Uncertainties

- Long-term event risk for lean LMHR people with very high ApoB on low-carb diets
- Whether low TG/HDL and low insulin materially reduce risk per ApoB particle
- Whether large buoyant LDL on keto has lower risk once ApoB particle number is high
- Whether imaging studies with CAC/CCTA are sensitive enough for early/progression risk in younger or low-risk cohorts
- How much of keto LDL response is saturated fat, low carbohydrate itself, weight loss phase, genetics, or energy trafficking
- Whether ApoB, LDL-P, non-HDL-C, Lp(a), hsCRP, HbA1c, blood pressure, and CAC/CCTA should be combined differently for this subgroup
