# Keto, low-carb high-fat, and where the blood markers come from

Follow-up to `cholesterol_cvd.md`. Status: 2026-06-16. Population evidence synthesis, not personal medical advice.

## Bottom line

A lipid or metabolic panel does not show "chemicals from keto" directly. It shows traffic between intestine, liver, adipose tissue, muscle, pancreas, and blood.

The simple model:

- dietary fat first travels mostly in chylomicrons from the intestine
- liver-made VLDL is remodeled into remnants, IDL, and LDL
- LDL-C is cholesterol mass inside LDL particles, not a direct count of particles
- ApoB is closer to the number of atherogenic particles
- glucose and insulin mostly reflect carbohydrate availability, pancreatic response, and insulin sensitivity
- ketones reflect liver conversion of fatty-acid-derived acetyl-CoA under low-insulin, low-carbohydrate conditions
- free fatty acids reflect adipose fat release, liver uptake, and tissue fat burning
- inflammation/metabolic markers reflect the person, not just the diet label

The usual keto pattern is lower triglycerides, higher HDL-C, lower glucose/insulin exposure, measurable ketones, higher fat flux, and variable LDL-C/ApoB. The usual low-carb high-fat pattern is similar but weaker if carbohydrate intake is not low enough to sustain ketosis. The usual ordinary mixed-diet pattern is low ketones, higher post-meal glucose/insulin, and lipid markers that depend heavily on weight, insulin resistance, saturated fat, fiber, genetics, and medication.

The important uncertainty is LDL/ApoB on keto. Some people see little change or improvement in ApoB/non-HDL. Some see large LDL-C and ApoB increases, especially in leaner low-carb responders or with high saturated fat. Low triglycerides and high HDL-C do not prove that high ApoB is harmless.

## Diet labels

Ordinary mixed diet means enough carbohydrate to keep post-meal glucose and insulin active and to prevent sustained nutritional ketosis. It can be healthy or unhealthy depending on food quality, energy balance, fiber, alcohol, adiposity, and metabolic disease.

Low-carb high-fat means carbohydrate is reduced and fat supplies more calories. It may or may not be ketogenic. A person eating 80-130 g/day carbohydrate may be "low carb" by trial definitions but often will not show sustained blood ketones.

Keto means carbohydrate is low enough, long enough, to shift fuel use toward fat oxidation and ketone production. In research, very-low-carbohydrate diets are often around 20-50 g/day carbohydrate or less than 10% of calories. Blood beta-hydroxybutyrate above about 0.5 mmol/L is often used as evidence that ketosis occurred, but ketone level varies with calories, protein, exercise, adaptation, fasting duration, and diabetes physiology.

The diet label is less informative than the setup:

- grams of carbohydrate
- saturated versus unsaturated fat
- dietary fiber and plant foods
- total energy balance and weight change
- baseline adiposity and insulin resistance
- thyroid, kidney, liver, pregnancy, menopause, and illness status
- alcohol, sleep, training load, and medications
- genetics, including LDL receptor/ApoE/Lp(a) background

## Marker-by-marker source map

LDL-C:

- biochemical source/pathway: LDL is mostly a downstream particle made from liver-secreted VLDL after triglyceride removal; it is not swallowed LDL from food
- measured marker: LDL-C estimates cholesterol mass inside LDL particles
- ordinary mixed diet: LDL-C depends strongly on saturated fat intake, unsaturated-fat replacement, body weight, thyroid status, genetics, and LDL receptor clearance
- keto: LDL-C can fall, stay similar, or rise; large rises are well described in some carbohydrate-restricted responders
- LCHF but not keto: direction depends on whether the diet mainly lowers weight/insulin resistance or mainly adds saturated fat without enough carbohydrate restriction to change TG-rich lipoprotein traffic
- person-level variability: LDL-C can be discordant from ApoB, so LDL-C alone can mislead in either direction

ApoB and non-HDL-C:

- biochemical source/pathway: fasting ApoB mostly reflects liver-derived ApoB-100 particles: VLDL, IDL, LDL, and Lp(a); intestinal chylomicrons and chylomicron remnants carry ApoB-48 after fat-containing meals
- measured marker: ApoB estimates particle number; non-HDL-C estimates cholesterol carried by all atherogenic non-HDL particles
- ordinary mixed diet: insulin resistance often increases VLDL/remnants and can leave LDL-C deceptively ordinary while ApoB/non-HDL stays high
- keto: triglyceride-rich particles often fall, but ApoB can rise if LDL particle number rises
- LCHF but not keto: ApoB/non-HDL depends on both remnant reduction and LDL-particle increase
- person-level variability: if LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and ApoB disagree, risk generally tracks ApoB or non-HDL better than LDL-C alone

Triglycerides:

- biochemical source/pathway: fasting triglycerides mostly reflect VLDL from the liver; after meals, chylomicrons carry recently eaten fat
- measured marker: blood triglycerides are the triglyceride cargo in circulating lipoproteins
- ordinary mixed diet: high refined carbohydrate, alcohol, excess calories, fatty liver, and insulin resistance tend to raise liver VLDL output
- keto: fasting triglycerides commonly fall because carbohydrate-driven liver triglyceride production and insulin resistance often improve
- LCHF but not keto: triglycerides often fall if weight and insulin resistance improve, but the effect is smaller and less reliable than with stricter carbohydrate restriction
- person-level variability: triglycerides move with recent alcohol, recent meals, weight loss, diabetes control, exercise, and liver fat

HDL-C:

- biochemical source/pathway: HDL participates in cholesterol efflux and lipid exchange, but HDL-C is cholesterol cargo, not direct HDL function
- measured marker: HDL-C is cholesterol mass inside HDL particles
- ordinary mixed diet: insulin resistance and high triglycerides often pair with lower HDL-C
- keto: HDL-C often rises, partly because triglyceride-rich lipoprotein traffic falls and fat intake rises
- LCHF but not keto: HDL-C may rise modestly, especially with weight loss and lower triglycerides
- person-level variability: very high HDL-C does not cancel high ApoB; HDL-C is a marker, not a guarantee of protective HDL function

Glucose:

- biochemical source/pathway: blood glucose comes from dietary carbohydrate absorption, liver glycogen breakdown, and liver/kidney gluconeogenesis
- measured marker: fasting glucose, post-meal glucose, and HbA1c measure different time windows of glycemia
- ordinary mixed diet: glucose rises after carbohydrate-containing meals, then insulin helps move glucose into tissues
- keto: post-meal glucose excursions usually shrink; fasting glucose may fall in insulin-resistant people but can remain normal or sometimes be higher, so HbA1c, post-meal data, sleep, stress, medication, and diabetes context matter
- LCHF but not keto: usually smaller glucose excursions than a high-refined-carbohydrate diet, proportional to carbohydrate amount and food matrix
- person-level variability: baseline diabetes status dominates; medication changes and weight loss can matter more than the diet label

Insulin:

- biochemical source/pathway: pancreatic beta cells secrete insulin in response to glucose, amino acids, incretin signals, and energy state
- measured marker: fasting insulin, C-peptide, post-meal insulin, and HOMA-IR are related but not identical
- ordinary mixed diet: insulin rises after carbohydrate-containing meals and suppresses adipose lipolysis
- keto: fasting and post-meal insulin usually fall when carbohydrate and energy intake fall
- LCHF but not keto: insulin exposure usually falls compared with higher-carbohydrate diets if carbohydrate is meaningfully lower
- person-level variability: insulin resistance, pancreatic beta-cell capacity, diabetes duration, sleep, stress, and medications change the result

Ketones:

- biochemical source/pathway: liver mitochondria convert fatty-acid-derived acetyl-CoA into acetoacetate and beta-hydroxybutyrate when carbohydrate/insulin availability is low
- measured marker: blood beta-hydroxybutyrate is the most useful nutritional-ketosis marker; urine ketones are delayed and less precise
- ordinary mixed diet: ketones are usually low except during overnight fasting, prolonged exercise, fasting, illness, or alcohol-related states
- keto: beta-hydroxybutyrate usually becomes measurable after days of carbohydrate restriction and can fluctuate across the day
- LCHF but not keto: ketones may remain low if carbohydrate or protein intake is high enough; high fat intake can blunt fasting-like ketone levels but does not reliably prevent ketosis when carbohydrate is very low
- person-level variability: nutritional ketosis is not diabetic ketoacidosis; ketoacidosis involves pathologic ketone accumulation plus acidosis, commonly with severe insulin deficiency

Free fatty acids:

- biochemical source/pathway: adipose triglycerides are hydrolyzed into free fatty acids and glycerol; free fatty acids go to liver and muscle for oxidation, re-esterification, VLDL production, or ketogenesis
- measured marker: plasma free fatty acids/non-esterified fatty acids are a flux marker, not a stored-fat measurement
- ordinary mixed diet: insulin after meals suppresses adipose lipolysis, so free fatty acids usually fall after eating
- keto: lower insulin permits more fat release and oxidation; free fatty-acid flux rises, especially during adaptation or calorie deficit
- LCHF but not keto: free fatty-acid flux may rise less, depending on carbohydrate level and energy balance
- person-level variability: high free fatty acids can mean fasting, exercise, keto adaptation, calorie deficit, or adipose insulin resistance; context is essential

Inflammation and metabolic markers:

- biochemical source/pathway: hs-CRP, white blood cell count, fibrinogen, ALT, GGT, uric acid, blood pressure, HbA1c, and HOMA-IR come from immune tone, liver fat, glycation, kidney handling, vascular tone, adiposity, and medication effects
- measured marker: these are downstream state markers, not diet-origin chemicals
- ordinary mixed diet: adverse markers often track visceral adiposity, fatty liver, insulin resistance, poor sleep, alcohol, smoking, infection, and inactivity
- keto: in T2D/obesity trials, HbA1c, fasting glucose, triglycerides, weight, and insulin-resistance markers often improve at 6 months; CRP effects are less consistent
- LCHF but not keto: similar direction if weight and glycemia improve, weaker if adherence is low or saturated fat displaces fiber-rich foods
- person-level variability: inflammation can improve while ApoB worsens, or ApoB can improve while glucose remains abnormal; one marker family cannot stand in for all the others

## Typical setups

Ordinary mixed diet:

- expected ketones: low most of the time
- expected glucose/insulin: higher post-meal swings than keto, especially after refined carbohydrate
- expected triglycerides: normal if metabolically healthy; higher with insulin resistance, fatty liver, alcohol, or excess calories
- expected HDL-C: lower when triglycerides and insulin resistance are high
- expected LDL-C/ApoB: depends on saturated fat, body weight, genetics, thyroid, LDL receptor clearance, and remnant burden
- main uncertainty: "ordinary" includes both high-fiber unsaturated-fat patterns and refined-carbohydrate/high-saturated-fat patterns, so the label itself predicts little

Keto:

- expected ketones: measurable beta-hydroxybutyrate if carbohydrate restriction is sufficient
- expected glucose/insulin: lower post-meal glucose and insulin exposure; largest improvement in people with insulin resistance or T2D
- expected triglycerides: often lower
- expected HDL-C: often higher
- expected free fatty acids: higher fat flux, especially early or in calorie deficit
- expected LDL-C/ApoB: variable; can rise substantially in some people
- main uncertainty: hard-outcome evidence is lacking for long-term keto with very high LDL-C/ApoB but low TG, high HDL-C, low glucose, and low insulin

Low-carb high-fat but not keto:

- expected ketones: low or intermittent unless carbohydrate is low enough for sustained ketosis
- expected glucose/insulin: lower than a higher-carbohydrate diet if carbohydrate is meaningfully reduced
- expected triglycerides: often lower, but less predictably than strict keto
- expected HDL-C: often modestly higher
- expected LDL-C/ApoB: depends strongly on fat quality, saturated fat load, fiber, calories, and weight change
- main uncertainty: many studies and self-reports mix low-carb, very-low-carb, ketogenic, high-saturated-fat, and unsaturated-fat versions, so subgroup inference is weak

## How to interpret a keto-looking panel

Low triglycerides plus high HDL-C usually says carbohydrate/remnant metabolism improved. It does not answer whether ApoB particle number is high.

High LDL-C on keto can mean more cholesterol per LDL particle, more LDL particles, slower LDL clearance, more VLDL-to-LDL conversion, or some mixture. ApoB or LDL-P is needed to separate particle number from cholesterol cargo.

Low glucose and low insulin usually say glycemic load or insulin resistance improved. They do not prove arterial-wall exposure to ApoB particles is harmless.

High ketones usually say carbohydrate availability is low enough for liver ketogenesis. They do not prove cardiovascular benefit or harm by themselves.

Inflammation markers can move independently. Weight loss can lower hs-CRP while high saturated fat raises LDL-C/ApoB. Infection can raise hs-CRP while the diet did nothing. A single marker should not be treated as the whole physiology.

## Uncertainty

Well supported:

- chylomicrons carry newly eaten fat from intestine
- VLDL is liver-made and can be remodeled into LDL
- ApoB is a better particle-burden marker than LDL-C when markers disagree
- low-carb diets can improve triglycerides, glycemia, weight, and insulin-resistance markers over months in T2D studies
- LDL-C/ApoB response to keto is heterogeneous

Less settled:

- whether long-term very high LDL-C/ApoB on keto is offset by low TG, high HDL-C, low glucose, low insulin, and low inflammation
- which keto version matters most: saturated fat, unsaturated fat, fiber, energy deficit, protein amount, or carbohydrate threshold
- how to generalize from T2D/obesity trials to lean, metabolically healthy keto users
- how durable the short-term metabolic benefits are after 12 months

Practical synthesis: keto and LCHF change the source mix of circulating fuels. They usually shift the body away from glucose/insulin traffic and toward fatty-acid/ketone traffic. That often improves triglycerides and glycemia. It can also raise LDL-C and ApoB in some people. Those are separate facts, not contradictions.
